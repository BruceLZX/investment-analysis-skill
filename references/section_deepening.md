## Company Overview

- Map legal entity, operating entity, product entity, and brand if they differ.
- Build a timeline of founding, pivots, product launches, financing, regulatory milestones, and customer traction.
- Explain customer, job-to-be-done, product, buyer, user, and budget owner.
- Identify whether the company is product-led, sales-led, hardware-led, service-led, regulatory-led, or ecosystem-led.

## Founders and Team

- Verify career chronology and role scope, not just employer brand names.
- Assess founder-market fit: why this team is unusually qualified for this problem.
- Separate technical invention, product execution, sales execution, fundraising ability, and organizational leadership.
- Look for gaps that must be hired around: CFO, enterprise sales, regulatory, manufacturing, security, clinical, supply chain.
- Compare claimed accomplishments with public records and prior company outcomes.

## Technology and Product

- Describe product architecture or workflow, not just product categories.
- Identify the bottleneck the technology solves and what must be true for it to remain advantaged.
- Distinguish real defensibility from implementation effort: IP, proprietary data, integrations, model performance, certification, switching costs, manufacturing process know-how.
- Assess build-vs-buy dependencies, third-party APIs, cloud costs, open-source reliance, hardware supply constraints, and technical debt.
- For frontier or hard-tech projects, state technology readiness, validation evidence, test conditions, and scale-up risk.

## Market and Industry

- Use two market-sizing approaches when possible: top-down external estimate and bottom-up customer/unit estimate.
- Separate TAM, SAM, SOM, and near-term obtainable revenue.
- Identify budget owner, replacement budget, procurement cycle, willingness to pay, and adoption friction.
- Analyze why now, what changed, and what adoption catalyst exists.
- Stress-test market size against comparable adoption curves and regulatory or infrastructure constraints.
- If international markets are mentioned, separate global TAM from reachable geography and explain what unlocks each market: customer access, GTM, localization, regulatory approval, supply chain, pricing, and service coverage.
- If policy is material, separate policy-created demand from organic demand and test whether government budgets, subsidies, or mandates are durable.

## Competition

- Include direct competitors, substitutes, incumbents, internal build, and non-consumption.
- Compare competitors by customer segment, price, product capability, distribution, integration depth, switching cost, and funding.
- State which competitor is most dangerous and why.
- Identify the company's wedge and whether it can expand into a durable position.
- Avoid 2x2 matrices unless axes are measurable and decision-relevant.
- Do not list foreign or adjacent companies as direct competitors unless they compete for the same buyer, budget, use case, geography, and procurement path.
- If policy/geopolitics is material, compare local champions, foreign entrants, restricted suppliers, protected incumbents, and standards-setting power.

## Geographic Market Context

- Use `geo_market_context_lens.md` when geography, jurisdiction, customer market, suppliers, channels, localization, foreign comparables, capital markets, or exit paths affect the thesis.
- Map home market, current revenue market, target expansion market, supplier/infrastructure market, comparable-company market, capital market, exit market, and investor perspective.
- Explain how geography changes demand, GTM, pricing, unit economics, competition, valuation comps, financing, risk, and exit.
- Label comparable companies as direct competitors, local substitutes, international competitors, strategic comparables, future entrants, or non-comparable.

## Business Model

- Show how money flows: buyer, user, pricing metric, contract length, gross margin drivers, cash collection, renewal/repurchase.
- SaaS: ARR/MRR, retention, CAC payback, sales cycle, ACV, gross margin, burn multiple.
- Marketplace: take rate, liquidity, frequency, GMV quality, subsidy reliance, supply/demand acquisition.
- Hardware: BOM, gross margin at scale, warranty, installation/service cost, working capital, supply chain.
- Biotech/hard-tech: milestone financing, regulatory path, capex, pilot-to-commercial conversion, strategic buyer logic.

## Financials

- Reconstruct available financials from filings, pitch materials, interviews, hiring, customer counts, pricing, and comparable benchmarks.
- Tie revenue growth to drivers: price, volume, retention, expansion, geography, channel mix.
- Analyze burn, runway, financing need, margin structure, working capital, and capital intensity.
- Run base/upside/downside sensitivity and state the variable that matters most.
- Treat missing financials as risk, not an empty section.

## Funding and Valuation

- Build financing chronology with amount, date, round, investors, valuation if public, and implied dilution when possible.
- Compare valuation using relevant comps: revenue multiple, ARR multiple, GMV multiple, transaction comps, replacement cost, or milestone value.
- Explain whether valuation requires flawless execution or allows margin of safety.
- Identify investor quality, signaling risk, follow-on capacity, liquidation preferences, and option pool implications when known.

## Strategy

- Convert strategy into milestones: product, customer, revenue, regulatory, hiring, geography, capital.
- Test whether strategy matches resources and capabilities.
- Identify second growth curves and whether they are logical adjacencies or distractions.
- Explain what must be true in 12 months for next financing or exit path to remain credible.

## Risk

- Rank risks by probability, impact, timing, detectability, and mitigation cost.
- Separate fatal risks from manageable execution risks.
- Link each risk to a milestone or evidence request.
- Include unknown-unknown areas when information is unusually thin.

## Policy / Geopolitics

- Use `policy_geopolitics_lens.md` when national strategy, regulation, government procurement, export controls, sanctions, data security, or cross-border supply chain affects the company.
- Start by mapping jurisdiction and viewpoint: company home market, operating markets, customer markets, supplier markets, financing/listing venue, likely acquirer market, and whether the reader is domestic or international.
- Determine whether policy is a demand driver, moat, catalyst, compliance cost, valuation discount, or thesis-breaking risk.
- Check primary policy sources where possible: laws, official plans, procurement notices, export-control lists, sanctions lists, standards bodies, regulatory approvals, official budgets, filings.
- Connect policy findings to market size, competition, supply chain, financials, risk, valuation, and exit.

## Investment Thesis

- Write both bull case and bear case.
- State the price/terms under which the investment becomes attractive or unattractive.
- Identify the non-consensus insight if any.
- Show the decision tree: invest now, wait for milestone, pass, or invest with conditions.

## Exit Strategy

- Identify actual likely acquirers or IPO markets, not generic "IPO/M&A".
- Compare with historical exits by size, growth, margin, technology, regulatory status, or strategic fit.
- Explain who would buy the company, why, when, and for which asset: customers, technology, team, license, data, distribution, or capacity.
- State expected holding period and return sensitivity to entry valuation.
- If policy/geopolitics is material, test foreign buyer limits, national-security review, sanctions exposure, listing venue constraints, and state-backed buyer logic.

## ESG and Governance

- Focus on financially material ESG and governance issues.
- Assess data privacy, labor practices, environmental impact, board control, related-party transactions, transparency, and compliance culture.
- Identify governance rights or information rights an investor should request.

## Scoring and Recommendation

- Scores must be justified by evidence, not intuition.
- For each score, include the top reason it is high or low.
- Show how recommendation changes under downside assumptions.
- End with specific next diligence actions and investment conditions.
