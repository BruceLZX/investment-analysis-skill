# Decision Framework

Use this file before drafting the executive briefing, investment thesis, recommendation, scoring, or final decision. Do not force precision when evidence does not support it.

## Contents

- Consensus / Non-Consensus View
- Scenario Analysis
- Key Assumptions
- Historical Analogs
- Capital Efficiency
- Position Sizing / Deal Sizing
- The One Question
- Three Takeaways

## Consensus / Non-Consensus View

State what the market, comparable investors, or the company's own pitch appears to assume. Then state whether the report has a differentiated view.

If there is no non-consensus insight, say the thesis is consensus and explain what that means for return expectations, valuation discipline, or margin of safety. Do not invent a variant perception for appearance.

## Scenario Analysis

Use bull/base/bear cases with explicit drivers and approximate probabilities.

- For public equities and growth companies, scenarios may use revenue, margin, multiple, cash flow, valuation, catalyst timing, or downside drawdown.
- For early-stage, biotech, hard-tech, and data-poor cases, use milestone scenarios such as product-market fit, regulatory readout, manufacturing yield, customer conversion, financing survival, or exit path.

Each scenario should include:

- Specific driver or failure mode.
- Evidence that makes it plausible.
- Financial or milestone implication.
- Investor implication.

For public equities, build scenarios from explicit financial drivers where data allows: revenue growth, segment mix, gross/operating margin, tax, share count, capex, working capital, free cash flow, net cash/debt, and valuation multiple. Include current share price context before stating upside/downside.

## Key Assumptions

Rank the 3-5 assumptions that most affect the decision. For each, state:

- Confidence.
- What would disprove it.
- Impact if wrong.
- Early warning sign.

This is often more useful than adding more facts.

## Historical Analogs

Use historical analogs only when they are close enough to teach a decision rule. A good analog matches on business model, market structure, stage, regulation, customer behavior, or capital intensity.

If no close analog exists, say so.

## Capital Efficiency

Use a capital efficiency scorecard when the company has raised material capital and data exists. Useful metrics include burn multiple, revenue per dollar raised, runway, revenue per employee, capex to next milestone, and dilution efficiency.

If data is unavailable, state which missing data would be required.

For public equities, capital efficiency should use reported financials where available: ROIC, ROE, ROA, asset turnover, incremental margins, reinvestment rate, FCF conversion, dividend/buyback yield, dilution, and M&A returns. Tie these to management capital allocation quality.

## Position Sizing / Deal Sizing

Use position sizing for public equities, liquid secondaries, or explicit portfolio-allocation requests.

For public equities, position size must reflect valuation upside/downside, balance-sheet resilience, liquidity, accounting quality, catalyst timing, volatility, evidence confidence, and whether the downside is mark-to-market volatility or permanent capital impairment.

For primary-market deals, translate this into check size, valuation discipline, downside loss, reserve strategy, and conditions for follow-on investment.

## The One Question

Before finalizing, identify the single diligence question whose truthful answer would most change the investment decision. It must be specific, answerable in principle, and decision-changing.

## Three Takeaways

End with the three things the reader should remember. These should be decision statements, not generic summaries.
