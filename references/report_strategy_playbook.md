# Report Strategy Playbook

Use this playbook before drafting the report. The reader may not know the company or industry; the report must teach enough context for a professional investment decision in either primary-market or secondary-market settings.

## Reference-Inspired Pattern

Strong reports often do three things before the final recommendation:

1. Explain the industry map: technology routes, value chain, regulation, commercial model, and market structure.
2. Identify the decisive bottleneck: manufacturing, distribution, reimbursement, regulation, data, IP, supply chain, customer adoption, or capital intensity.
3. Place the company inside that map: why this company matters, what it controls, what it depends on, and what can break the thesis.

Do not force every company into the same chapter emphasis. Choose the analysis spine based on the company.

## Choose the Analysis Spine

Select 1-3 primary spines and make them visible in the report.

| Company type | Primary spine | Must answer |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Public equity / listed company | earnings quality, competitive position, valuation, catalysts, downside risk | Is the market mispricing future cash flow or risk? |
| Early-stage startup | team, problem urgency, product proof, market timing, fundraising path | Is there enough evidence to underwrite team and learning speed? |
| Growth-stage startup | unit economics, GTM repeatability, retention, margin, financing need | Can growth scale without destroying economics? |
| Deep tech / hard tech | technical feasibility, validation evidence, manufacturing, capex, regulation | What has been proven, under what conditions, and what remains unproven? |
| Biotech / medtech | mechanism, clinical data, regulatory path, reimbursement, IP, pipeline optionality | Is the clinical/regulatory risk priced correctly? |
| Industrial / hardware | BOM, supply chain, quality, installation/service economics, utilization | Can the company produce and support at scale? |
| Marketplace / platform | liquidity, network effects, take rate, cohort behavior, subsidy reliance | Is the marketplace becoming more efficient with scale? |
| Consumer / brand | customer insight, repeat behavior, channel economics, brand defensibility | Is demand durable or promotion-driven? |
| Infrastructure / tools | workflow lock-in, switching cost, integration depth, developer/operator adoption | Is this a must-have system of record/workflow or a feature? |
| Geographic / market-system-sensitive | home market, customer market, supplier ecosystem, GTM, pricing, foreign comparables, capital market, exit venue | Which geography or market system actually drives demand, economics, valuation, and exit? |
| Regulated / policy-driven | license, compliance, policy path, local execution, government/customer budget | Does policy create a durable opening or temporary subsidy? |
| Policy / geopolitics-sensitive | national strategy, export controls, sanctions, supply-chain sovereignty, government procurement, cross-border market access | Does policy create a moat/catalyst, or a hidden constraint on growth, valuation, and exit? |
| Special situations / distressed | asset value, liability structure, catalyst, restructuring path, management change | Is the discount to intrinsic value real and is there a catalyst to close it? |
| Spin-off / carve-out | standalone economics, dis-synergy cost, capital structure, management incentives, index inclusion | Will the spin-off re-rate as a pure-play and attract a new investor base? |
| Activist / control | governance gap, capital allocation, margin opportunity, board path, exit timeline | Can a minority investor force value-unlocking change? |
| Pre-IPO / cross-over | IPO readiness, last-round price, public comps, lock-up, index inclusion, cornerstone demand | Is the last private round price above or below where public markets will clear? |
| Crypto / Web3 | tokenomics, protocol revenue, treasury, governance, regulatory classification, ecosystem | Is value captured by the token or by the equity, and is that sustainable? |

## Reader Education Layer

For unfamiliar industries, include a compact learning layer before deep company judgment:

- What the industry does and why customers pay.
- The main technology or product routes and their tradeoffs.
- The value chain and where profit pools sit.
- The regulatory and reimbursement/payment path.
- The competitive map and likely winners/losers.
- The key metrics investors use in this industry.
- The 3-5 debates that determine the investment outcome.

This section should not become a textbook. Tie every explanation back to why it matters for the target company.

## Primary vs Secondary Market Adaptation

For primary-market deals, emphasize:

- Founder-market fit and reference checks.
- Product proof and customer validation.
- Financing need, runway, dilution, next-round milestones.
- Terms, valuation, liquidation preference, governance rights.
- What must be true before writing a check.

For secondary-market/public-equity analysis, emphasize:

- Revenue quality, margin structure, cash flow, balance sheet, guidance credibility.
- Consensus expectations vs. evidence.
- Valuation multiples, DCF/sensitivity, and catalyst calendar.
- Latest stock price, market cap, enterprise value when available, trading date/time, and currency.
- Professional financial statement analysis: income statement quality, margin drivers, recurring vs. non-recurring profit, three-statement linkage, cash conversion, working capital, capex, balance-sheet health, accounting notes, and capital allocation.
- Balance-sheet health: cash, debt, maturity profile, leverage, liquidity, asset quality, off-balance-sheet/contingent liabilities, and refinancing risk.
- Downside case, drawdown risk, liquidity, and position-sizing implication.
- Management capital allocation and disclosure quality.

If the company can be analyzed both ways, include both lenses and state which lens drives the recommendation.

## Report Architecture

Default full report structure:

1. 5-minute briefing: conclusion, score, action, key reasons, key risks.
2. Industry primer: enough context for a non-specialist reader.
3. Company map: legal entity, product, customer, business model, stage, timeline.
4. Thesis evidence: what must be true and the evidence for/against it.
5. Deep sections selected by company type.
6. Financial/valuation work adjusted for primary or secondary market.
7. Risk register with mitigants and evidence requests.
8. Investment decision: action, price/terms, conditions, and next diligence.

The existing 14-section framework is a coverage checklist, not a rigid outline. Merge, reorder, or expand sections when the company requires a different narrative, but still make sure all material coverage areas are addressed.

## Reading Experience

The report should feel like a professional investment memo with clear hierarchy, not a worksheet and not a wall of text.

Use narrative sections for the argument, then add compact visual elements where they reduce cognitive load:

- Executive briefing cards for top-level decision facts.
- KPI dashboards for 4-8 numbers that should be scanned together.
- Tables for peer comps, financial trends, milestone gates, valuation cases, and source-backed claim checks.
- Risk matrices and warning callouts only for decision-changing risks.
- Timelines for milestones, regulatory path, financing history, or catalyst calendar.
- Inline source/confidence tags near the claim they support.
- A consolidated source appendix with clickable links or local source paths, source type, confidence, and claim coverage.

Avoid two extremes:

- Do not repeat the same `judgement/evidence/mechanism/risk/implication` cards in every section.
- Do not repeat the same `投资人读法`, `来源/Source`, `数字锚点`, or `仍需确认` block in every section. It reads like a worksheet and should fail QA.
- Do not replace structure with long black-text paragraphs. Dense analysis should be broken with content-specific micro-headings, tables, KPI strips, or callouts.

## Minimum Decision Standard

At the end, the reader should be able to answer:

- What does the company actually do?
- Why does this industry matter now?
- What is the company’s unfair advantage, if any?
- What evidence supports the advantage?
- What could make the thesis wrong?
- What are the economics and valuation?
- What should an investor do now?
- What information is still missing before a real investment decision?

If the report cannot answer these, continue the investigation loop.

## KPI Selection

Use a compact KPI dashboard only when metrics are available or can be responsibly proxied. Select 4-8 metrics that actually change the investment decision; do not include a full checklist just because a company type has many possible metrics.

| Company type | Prefer these KPI families |
| --- | --- |
| Public equity | revenue quality, margin trend, FCF conversion, ROIC/WACC, leverage, consensus delta, catalyst/risk calendar |
| Early-stage startup | founder-market fit, product proof, customer validation, product velocity, burn/runway, next-round milestones |
| Growth-stage | NRR/retention, CAC payback, LTV:CAC, burn multiple, rule of 40, channel mix, management depth |
| Deep tech / hard tech | TRL, validation conditions, IP, yield/defect rate, BOM path, supply chain concentration, capex to scale |
| Biotech / medtech | mechanism, trial phase/endpoints, indication base rates, IP life, reimbursement, cash to next readout |
| Marketplace / platform | liquidity, take rate, cohort retention, contribution margin, subsidy reliance, multi-tenanting, network density |
| Consumer / brand | repeat purchase, CAC by channel, gross margin after returns/discounts, brand search, reviews, inventory turns |
| Industrial / hardware | utilization, installation/service cost, warranty, working capital, supplier concentration, production quality |
| Special situations | asset value, liability stack, catalyst timing, restructuring path, downside recovery, governance trigger |

If the relevant KPI is unavailable, write the missing metric as an explicit diligence request instead of filling the dashboard with weaker substitutes.

## Public Equity Financials Lens Selection

Before drafting any listed-company or secondary-market report, read `references/public_equity_financials_lens.md`. This is mandatory when the request includes a ticker/security code, share price, stock-buying decision, public company, A-share/H-share/US-listed company, or liquid secondary-market position.

Use the lens to decide which financial statement and market-data modules drive the recommendation:

- Current market data: ticker, exchange, currency, latest price, market cap, enterprise value, trading timestamp.
- Financial statements: latest annual report plus latest interim/quarterly report where available.
- Earnings quality: revenue drivers, segment mix, margin trend, non-recurring items, accounting quality, tax, EPS/share count.
- Cash flow: operating cash flow, capex, free cash flow, working-capital movements, cash conversion, financing cash flow.
- Balance sheet and notes: cash/debt, maturity wall, interest coverage, liquidity, asset quality, contingent/off-balance-sheet obligations, related-party transactions, restricted/pledged assets, auditor issues, impairments, provisions, and key accounting estimates.
- Valuation: multiples, peer/historical comparison, DCF or scenario valuation where evidence supports it.
- Expectations: consensus/guidance/profit warnings/order backlog if available.
- Portfolio action: Buy/Add/Hold/Watchlist/Reduce/Sell/Avoid, time horizon, catalyst calendar, and position sizing.

For public equities, do not make a buy/sell recommendation before the report has considered whether assets and liabilities are healthy under the bear case.

## Geographic Market Context Lens Selection

Before drafting market size, GTM, business model, competition, valuation comps, risks, or exit, decide whether `references/geo_market_context_lens.md` is material. It is usually material when the company, customers, suppliers, competitors, comparables, investors, listing venue, or exit buyers sit in different countries or regions.

If material, map home market, current revenue market, target expansion market, supplier/infrastructure market, comparable-company market, capital market, exit market, and investor perspective. Then use that map in TAM/SAM/SOM, GTM, pricing, supply chain, competition, valuation, risk, and exit.

## Policy / Geopolitics Lens Selection

Before drafting, decide whether `references/policy_geopolitics_lens.md` is material. It is usually material for aerospace, defense, semiconductors, AI, energy, biotech/medtech, telecom, cybersecurity, data infrastructure, finance, transportation, critical minerals, advanced manufacturing, government procurement, and cross-border supply chains.

If material, first choose the relevant jurisdictions and investor perspective: company home market, revenue market, supplier market, financing/listing market, likely acquirer market, and whether the report is written from a domestic or international investor's view. Do not apply China, US, or Europe policy logic to all companies by default.

Then integrate policy into the thesis: market timing, customer budgets, competitor access, supply-chain constraints, regulatory path, valuation discount/premium, and exit limitations. If not material, state briefly that no policy/geopolitical factor appears decision-changing.
