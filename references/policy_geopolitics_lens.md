# Policy and Geopolitics Lens

Use this file when policy, national strategy, international relations, regulation, government procurement, export controls, sanctions, data security, strategic industries, or cross-border supply chains can materially affect market size, competition, valuation, risk, or exit.

Do not impose one country's policy logic on every company. First identify the relevant jurisdictions and the report's investment perspective.

## Trigger Conditions

Read this lens when any of these are true:

- The company operates in a regulated or license-dependent market.
- The industry is strategic: aerospace, defense, semiconductors, AI, energy, biotech, medtech, telecom, cybersecurity, data infrastructure, finance, transportation, agriculture, critical minerals, or advanced manufacturing.
- Customers, revenue, subsidies, procurement, or approvals depend on government budgets or policy continuity.
- The product is dual-use, export-controlled, sanction-sensitive, or dependent on foreign components, cloud, models, chips, instruments, materials, data, or IP.
- Overseas expansion, listing, M&A, financing, supply chain, or exit depends on international market access.

## Jurisdiction and Perspective Setup

Before analysis, map:

- **Company base**: incorporation, headquarters, R&D, manufacturing, data hosting, operating licenses.
- **Revenue markets**: current customers, target customers, government or enterprise budget owners.
- **Supply chain**: critical suppliers, components, cloud/models/chips, instruments, IP, and data flows.
- **Capital and exit path**: investor jurisdiction, listing venue, likely acquirers, foreign-investment review exposure.
- **Reader perspective**: domestic investor, international investor, strategic buyer, public-market investor, or primary-market investor.

Then choose the lens:

| Situation | Primary policy lens |
| --- | --- |
| China company serving China market | central policy, local implementation, procurement, industrial catalogs, subsidies, data/security reviews, domestic substitution, local-government payment risk |
| China company going global | China outbound rules plus target-market access, sanctions/export controls, tariffs, foreign-investment review, overseas compliance, brand/geopolitical perception |
| US company | federal/state regulation, procurement, export controls, CFIUS, antitrust, IRA/CHIPS/health/defense/data rules as relevant, international sales restrictions |
| European company | EU-level regulation, member-state implementation, GDPR/data, CE/MDR/EMA/energy/AI Act as relevant, state aid, procurement, trade and supply-chain rules |
| Cross-border supply chain | home market + supplier market + customer market + financing/listing market + likely acquirer market |
| Global category leader | compare US/EU/China policy regimes and ask which regime creates advantage, compliance cost, or market-access constraint |

If the company location is uncertain, state the uncertainty and analyze the jurisdictions that could materially change the conclusion.

## Analysis Questions

Answer only the questions that matter for the company:

1. **National / regional strategy fit**: Is the company aligned with national, regional, state, EU-level, or international policy priorities? Is policy support structural demand or temporary subsidy?
2. **Regulatory path**: What licenses, approvals, certifications, safety reviews, reimbursement rules, data rules, or procurement qualifications are required?
3. **Government customer / budget**: Who controls the budget, how long is procurement, how reliable is payment, and can policy changes delay revenue?
4. **International exposure**: What export controls, sanctions, tariffs, foreign investment reviews, data-transfer rules, or market-access barriers matter?
5. **Supply-chain sovereignty**: Which components, chips, instruments, materials, software, cloud services, or IP create choke points?
6. **Standards and certification**: Does the company benefit from or depend on technical standards, industry certifications, safety regimes, or international bodies?
7. **Policy upside vs. policy trap**: Does policy create a moat/catalyst, or does it inflate TAM, invite crowded competition, and reduce pricing power?
8. **Investment implication**: How should policy change valuation, timeline, probability of milestones, required diligence, downside case, and exit route?

## Evidence Sources

Prefer primary or near-primary sources:

- Laws, regulations, regulatory drafts, enforcement actions, licenses, procurement notices, official budgets, government plans, industry catalogs, standards bodies, export-control lists, sanctions lists, customs/tariff rules, official statistical releases, and stock-exchange filings.
- For China-related analysis, check central policy, provincial/local implementation, procurement catalogs, subsidies, standards, industrial parks, data/security review, and domestic-substitution rules separately. Local support is not the same as national demand.
- For US-related analysis, separate federal and state rules; check agency guidance, procurement, export controls, sanctions, CFIUS, antitrust, sector regulators, and subsidy/industrial-policy programs when relevant.
- For Europe-related analysis, separate EU-level regulation from member-state implementation; check GDPR/data transfer, product certification, sector regulators, state aid, procurement, and EU trade/supply-chain rules when relevant.
- For international exposure, compare the rules in the company's home market, target market, supplier market, financing/listing market, and likely acquirer market.

## Report Integration

Do not write a generic policy essay. Integrate findings into:

- Market and industry: TAM/SAM/SOM, adoption timing, subsidy dependence, government budget, replacement cycle.
- Competition: protected incumbents, national champions, local-content rules, foreign competitors blocked or advantaged.
- Product and technology: certification, safety, dual-use constraints, standards, export-control sensitivity.
- Business model and financials: procurement cycle, payment risk, margin pressure from policy pricing, capex/subsidy dependency.
- Risk: policy reversal, compliance breach, sanctions, data-security review, local-government payment delay, supply-chain interruption.
- Valuation and exit: policy-driven discount/premium, IPO/M&A restrictions, foreign buyer limits, lock-up or national-security review.

If the lens is not material, state briefly: `政策/地缘因素：未发现对投资结论有重大影响的政策或跨境约束。`
